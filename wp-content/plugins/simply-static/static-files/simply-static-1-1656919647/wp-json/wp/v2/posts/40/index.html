JUST AS WE thought that monkeypox<\/a> would be the new virus<\/a> scare for 2022, the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) declared a national incident of repeated poliovirus detection in sewage<\/a> in north and east London.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Repeated positive readings for polio<\/a> suggest that there is an ongoing infection and likely transmission in the area. This is unexpected since the UK has been declared polio-free since 2003. Here\u2019s what you need to know.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

WHAT IS POLIO?<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

Poliomyelitis<\/a> (polio) is a devastating disease that historically has caused paralysis and death around the world. It is caused by polioviruses, small RNA viruses that can damage cells in the nervous system.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

It is not found in animals, so, like smallpox, it can be eradicated. And thanks to effective vaccination campaigns<\/a>, we have been getting closer to this goal every year.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

There are three types<\/a> of poliovirus, and infection or immunization by one type does not protect against another. Type 1 poliovirus has continued to cause outbreaks, but transmission by types 2 and 3 have been successfully interrupted by vaccination.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Poliovirus is transmitted by respiratory droplets, but it can also be caught from food or water that\u2019s been in contact with the feces of someone who has the virus.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

It can survive at normal temperatures for many days. The last remaining outbreaks have been associated with areas with poor sanitation that are hard to reach with vaccines. Afghanistan and Pakistan are the only two countries where wild polio is still endemic and are targeted by\u00a0eradication programs<\/a>\u00a0to stop the virus from spreading to other countries.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
